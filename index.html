Project Overview

TL;DR

Half of Americans follow specific diets, yet the Starbucks app lacks features to support dietary preferences. Our solution allows users to set their preferences, providing tailored recommendations throughout the menu and making customization easier and more intuitive.

Problem

According to a 2023 study by the International Food Information Council, 5 out of 10 Americans follow a specific eating pattern or diet.
For many, maintaining these dietary preferences comes with daily challenges—whether it’s grocery shopping, dining out, or grabbing their daily caffeine fix.

The Starbucks app, consistently ranked among the top 10 Food and Drink apps on the App Store, is celebrated for its convenience, especially when it comes to ordering customized drinks ahead of time.

However, despite offering extensive customization options, the app provides little guidance and lacks features to set or save dietary preferences.

Solution

We addressed dietary uncertainty with a Taste Preferences Setting, a personalized 'For You' menu, and enhanced product pages with dietary conflict warnings, creating a more tailored and confident ordering experience.

Impact

Our solution empowers users to make confident, informed choices by aligning their dietary needs with tailored recommendations. This enhances user satisfaction, reduces decision fatigue, and positions Starbucks as a more inclusive and customer-focused brand.

Why Starbucks?

With nearly 40,000 locations worldwide, Starbucks remains as the #1 coffee chain in the world.
Renowned for its premium coffee, handcrafted beverages, and personalized service, it boasts a loyal customer base and a consistent global presence. 
Customers choose Starbucks for its high-quality, customizable drinks and comfortable, convenient experience, and the app is a huge part of that for many Starbucks drinkers.
With an app that allows you to fine-tune every drink on their already large menu, we chose this app to explore the complexities users face in the customization process

We interviewed 9 coffee drinkers.

  • A mix of Starbucks and non-Starbucks coffee drinkers
  • Both app users and non-app users
  • Shared commonality: All participants mentioned they have specific taste preferences or dietary restrictions, as well as feeling a lack of guidance on what to order.

As our research deepened, it became clear that most users felt one thing:
Overwhelmed.

A quick google search revealed it wasn't just our research - this theme is present across the internet.

While conducting competitor research, we were surprised to find that none of Starbucks’s competitors were effectively addressing the complex issue of reflecting dietary needs throughout their menus.

Through our initial interviews and desk research, we narrowed in on one opportunity:

Customers struggle to accommodate their dietary preferences, leading to uncertainty about their orders and increased cognitive load.

Our goal is to enhance the Starbucks app by enabling users to specify dietary preferences, easily customize their orders, and receive tailored recommendations, ensuring an intuitive and confidence-boosting experience.

Key moments: Opportunity Solution Tree

At the root of our tree is our desired outcome: create clarity for the drink ordering and customization process.

Based on the research and interviews we conducted, we identified 3 solutions.

Show recommended or popular ingredient choices during drink customization to reduce cognitive load.

Tag menu items with compatibility based on user preferences (e.g., “can be made dairy-free”).

Allow users to input preferences and receive tailored menu suggestions.

Key moments: Wireframes
Bringing the user flow to life, we created low-fi wireframes for the taste preferences settings. This step was essential to enable personalized recommendations on their 'For You' menu, as well as warn them when a drink has a dietary conflict.
ORIGINAL CONCEPT

Initially, we implemented a button-based selection system.

AFTER FEEDBACK

However, feedback revealed that checkbox inputs would provide a more intuitive and familiar experience for users, so we revised the design accordingly. Additionally, we relocated the 'preferences saved' notification to the top of the page for improved visibility and usability.

Bringing the user flow to life, we created low-fi wireframes for the taste preferences settings. This step was essential to enable personalized recommendations on their 'For You' menu, as well as warn them when a drink has a dietary conflict.
Next, we focused on the Product Description Page, aiming to create a seamless way to alert users of dietary conflicts. To address this, we introduced warning symbols and tooltips.
ORIGINAL CONCEPT

Initially, we experimented with placing these warnings both above the ingredient drop-downs and beside specific ingredients.

AFTER FEEDBACK

However, feedback indicated that this approach made the page feel cluttered. In response, we simplified the design by using the color red to outline the dropdown and repositioned the warning symbols exclusively next to the relevant ingredients. This helped maintain clarity while still effectively communicating critical information to users.

Once we completed our official prototype, we conducted usability tests with 6 participants.
Our goal was to understand how easily users can set dietary preferences and how it affects their experience ordering.

Our main takeaways:
  • Users need an easy way to identify what fits their dietary preferences so they can make informed decisions and avoid potential health risks.
  • Users increasingly desire personalized experiences and recommendations when ordering beverages like coffee.
  • Every user we talked with had their own taste preferences or dietary restrictions.
Usability Testing: Iterating based on user feedback
1. Home screen navigation: Additional paths to Menu
BEFORE & DURING TESTING

Finding: In the initial designs, the home screen ad components including “Order” CTAs were removed. User interviews indicated that these looked too much like ads. Usability testing proved that some users rely on the Order CTAs to move forward to the Menu.

IMPROVED DESIGN

Update: Added Order ad components back onto the home screen

2. Steps to Menu: Created a clearer path to order after setting preferences.
BEFORE & DURING TESTING

Finding: In the initial designs, the completed preference message appeared once users had chosen their dietary restrictions and preferences.  In usability testing, we saw that every one of our testers hit the ‘back’ arrow from this screen to get to the Menu to order which caused hesitation in what to  do next.

IMPROVED DESIGN

Update: Added a CTA that takes the user directly to their newly crafted “For You” menu as a way to show them their menu and get them ordering faster.

3. Content clarity: Removing a link and adding another.
BEFORE & DURING TESTING

Finding: 5/6 users thought that clicking “See all” would allow them to see all the drinks in that category, even though it meant all the drinks in that category in the submenu.

IMPROVED DESIGN

Update: Within the “For You” menu, we removed the “See all” link and added a clearer CTA at the end of each category to allow for more natural navigation from one menu to the other.

The Solution: Next Steps and Beyond

How can we take this even further? Through our research and user interviews, we discussed a lot of future possibilities.

Now
  • Consider how someone with multiple dietary preferences might see those displayed.
  • Consider inclusivity with all product offerings (food, etc.) in relation to dietary preferences.
  • Research and analyze the barista’s role in this transaction
Later
  • Consider layering a feature that would automatically customize products based on dietary preferences.
  • As users order more, recognize a pattern in what they order and create personalized categories, such as “Fruity & High Caffeine” or “Dairy-free Customer Favorites”
  • Consider adding or merging “Previously Ordered” category to the “For You” menu

What we learned

Process:

It’s easier to write something down and tweak it around than try to get it perfect the first time.

Have a reason why:

All design decisions should have a clear purpose, and you should be able to communicate that purpose.

Common threads are key:

Recognizing patterns and commonalities allows for stronger design decisions.

Pressure Testing:

Pressure testing: You can try to anticipate what users actions will be, but more often than not users will surprise you.

Impact of a design decision:

When removing a component, it's crucial to fully understand its functionality and impact.

Content Strategy:

Changing the context of a component can change its meaning to the user.
